The plan you saw on Connect for Health Colorado has been discontinued.

The Silver $1500 plan you browsed in January — with the $1,500 deductible, 50% coinsurance, and $8,000 max out-of-pocket — was removed from SelectHealth's 2026 lineup. It no longer exists on the marketplace.

The good news: the three replacement options below all use the same SelectHealth network and same doctors. Two of them can actually be much better than the old plan if your income qualifies for cost-sharing reductions. This guide walks through everything.

There are really only three decisions to make, and they build on each other in order. Get the first one right and the rest fall into place: 1) What's your income? 2) How much care do you expect to use? 3) When should you file your tax return?

Key Terms in Plain English

DeductibleHow much you pay before insurance kicks in. Copay visits and preventive care are exempt.
CopayA fixed amount per visit (e.g. $35 for a doctor visit). Paid regardless of deductible.
CoinsuranceAfter meeting the deductible, you split costs. "40%" means you pay 40%, insurance pays 60%.
Max Out-of-PocketYour ceiling for the year. After this, insurance covers 100%. Your financial safety net.
CSRCost-Sharing Reduction — lowers deductible, copays, and max OOP. Only on Silver plans. Income-dependent.
APTCFederal tax credit that lowers your monthly premium. Reconciled at tax time — accuracy matters.
CPAColorado Premium Assistance — $80/month from the state. NOT a tax credit. Never repaid.
EPOExclusive Provider Organization — must use in-network doctors except for emergencies.

The 2026 Landscape — What Changed

Federal enhanced subsidies expired December 31, 2025. From 2021 through 2025, Congress provided extra premium help that kept marketplace costs low. These were not renewed. Average net premiums roughly doubled for subsidized enrollees.

Colorado stepped in — partially. Colorado created Premium Assistance (CPA): $80/month applied automatically. It's not a tax credit — it never has to be repaid even if your income changes. It helps, but doesn't fully replace what expired.

Accuracy matters more than ever. Starting in 2026, the APTC repayment cap is removed. If you receive more premium tax credit than you're entitled to (because your income ends up higher than estimated), you must repay the full excess. Overestimating income is safer than underestimating.

How CSR Transforms Silver Plans

This is the single biggest thing most people miss. CSR (Cost-Sharing Reduction) is a free upgrade that only applies to Silver plans purchased through Connect for Health Colorado. It doesn't change your premium — it changes the plan itself: lower deductible, lower copays, lower max out-of-pocket.

Why Silver often beats Gold: At first glance, Gold looks better (lower deductible, lower coinsurance). But with CSR, a Silver plan can have a $0 deductible and $3,200 max OOP — far better than Gold's $1,500 deductible and $9,000 max OOP — while also costing less per month.

Decision 3: When to File Your Tax Return

The lever most people don't realize they have. Filing your 2025 tax return triggers the data match that starts Medicaid redetermination. Delay the filing = more months of free coverage before premiums begin. It doesn't change which plan is best — it changes how many months you pay for it.

What This Means in Dollars

If your net monthly premium is around $235 (at ~$42K income), every month on Medicaid instead of marketplace saves $235. Filing April 15 instead of February saves roughly $470. Filing on extension could save over $1,650 — but if you owe any tax, payment is still due April 15 even with an extension.

Recommendation: File around April 15. It keeps Medicaid through roughly June/July, avoids the complexity of an extension, and gives a clean Special Enrollment Period window in mid-summer. Start shopping plans at connectforhealthco.com before filing, so you're ready to enroll the moment the Medicaid termination notice arrives.

Important Caveats

Filing an extension is not the same as not filing. The extension only extends the filing deadline, not the payment deadline. Medicaid may also redetermine eligibility on its own through periodic data matches — delaying the filing doesn't guarantee continuation, it just makes it more likely.

What to Do Next

High Priority
  1. Estimate your 2026 income as precisely as possible. This determines your CSR tier, premium subsidy amount, and CPA eligibility. If income is variable, estimate conservatively — overestimating is safer than underestimating because the repayment cap is gone.
  2. Decide when to file your 2025 tax return. April 15 is the recommended sweet spot. Start shopping plans online before filing so you're ready to enroll quickly.
  3. Act immediately when the Medicaid termination notice arrives. You'll have a 60–90 day Special Enrollment Period. Set calendar reminders at 30 and 60 days. Missing the SEP means waiting until November 2026 open enrollment.
  4. Enroll through Connect for Health Colorado — not directly with SelectHealth. You must go through the marketplace to receive APTC, CSR, or CPA. Call 855-752-6749 or visit connectforhealthco.com.
Medium Priority
  1. Look up prescriptions on the SelectHealth formulary. Visit selecthealth.org/pharmacy and verify each medication is Tier 1 (generic = $0). If any are Tier 2+, factor copay differences between plans.
  2. Compare exact premiums using the Plan Finder. At connectforhealthco.com Plan Finder, enter your zip code, age 40, non-tobacco, and income for exact monthly premiums after all credits.
  3. Understand the EPO limitation. All three plans are EPO — no out-of-network coverage except true emergencies. If you travel frequently, only emergency situations are covered.
Good to Know
  1. Take advantage of $0 virtual visits. All SelectHealth plans include free virtual visits for primary care, urgent care, and mental health.
  2. Look into wellness rewards. SelectHealth offers up to $580/year in rewards for completing checkups, screenings, and health classes.
